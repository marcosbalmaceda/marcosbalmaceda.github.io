Belief Updating and AI Adoption by Entrepreneurs
with Juan Pedro Ronconi [JMP] (Manuscript's Target: Management Science)
Presented at: AFE 2026 (U. Chicago)†; BERD 2026 (KU Leuven)
Abstract IRB 1 IRB 2 Pre-registration 1 Pre-registration 2

Generative AI is freely available and raises productivity, yet entrepreneurs and small firms adopt it slowly and use it shallowly. A natural explanation is that entrepreneurs underestimate how much AI could improve their business, so correcting that belief should raise adoption. We test this in a pre-registered field experiment with 736 Chilean entrepreneurs, randomly assigned to informational vignettes depicting a fictional entrepreneur applying ChatGPT to her own business, or to an uninformative control. The vignettes correct beliefs. Profit expectations rise in a Bayesian pattern, concentrated among the most pessimistic participants. Yet behavior does not follow. Willingness to pay for personalized AI coaching and take-up of a free AI course are unchanged in the same session as the belief update—ruling out belief decay—and a four-month follow-up finds no effect on AI use, business practices, sales, or profits. The null holds even among the strongest updaters. Asked what holds them back, entrepreneurs name material constraints (money, time, and data privacy), not a failure to see AI’s relevance. Correcting profit beliefs is not enough to move adoption: that requires lowering the costs of acting—money, time, and the firm-specific work of putting a general-purpose tool to use—not only communicating value.

Platform Certification as a Substitute for Reputation: Evidence from Mandatory AI Disclosure.
with Michael Mödl (Under review at the Strategic Entrepreneurship Journal)
Presented at: DRUID26 (Copenhagen); AoM 2025 (Copenhagen); BERD 2025 (U. Namur)
Abstract Presentation

This study examines how mandatory disclosure policies affect entrepreneurial resource acquisition when traditional credibility signals vary. We propose a conditional substitution framework where platform certification replaces missing reputational signals. Exploiting Kickstarter’s mandatory AI use disclosure policy as a quasi-natural experiment across 27,845 projects, we find that mandatory disclosure significantly enhances funding outcomes for AI-using entrepreneurs overall, though effects vary substantially by entrepreneur type. First-time entrepreneurs lacking track records experience positive effects, while serial entrepreneurs with established reputations experience negative effects. While prior work demonstrates that signals typically complement one another, we provide empirical evidence that platform verification’s value depends critically on entrepreneurs’ existing signal portfolios. These findings identify boundary conditions to signal complementarity and reveal how governance design democratizes market access.

Technology Licensing and Outward Inventor Mobility: The Role of Relational Contracting.
with Bart Leten, Stijn Kelchtermans, & Eduardo Melero (Manuscript's Target: Strategic Management Journal)
Presented at: SMS 2026 (Berlin)†; DRUID26 (Copenhagen); EPIP 2025 (U. Antwerp); Data and Algorithms for ST&I Studies (KU Leuven)
🏅 Nominated for the SMS Annual Conference PhD Paper Prize (2026)
Abstract Presentation

Technology licensing and inventor mobility have largely been treated as parallel but separate channels: codified knowledge flows through contracts, tacit knowledge through careers. We develop a demand-side perspective linking these channels: licensing creates targeted demand for inventors at precisely one firm—the counterparty—and relational governance provisions substitute for such hiring by providing structured access to tacit knowledge. Using 3,558 pharmaceutical licensing agreements linked to inventor career histories, a staggered difference-in-differences design finds that outlicensing nearly doubles inventor mobility toward the licensee relative to other firms (18.6 vs. 10.4 percentage points). Relational governance provisions attenuate this targeted outflow by half while leaving other-firm mobility unaffected. These findings reveal a previously unrecognized human-capital consequence of licensing: contract design shapes post-agreement human capital distributions.

Shadow AI and Knowledge Governance in Firms
with Pablo Paniagua (Under review at Management Science)
Abstract

Organizational theory expects firms to decentralize authority when productive knowledge disperses to frontline workers. Generative AI has dispersed that knowledge to every knowledge worker, yet firms restrict access to AI, and workers conceal the gains they obtain even where access is allowed. We develop a formal model in which both responses are rational equilibria rather than governance mistakes. IT restricts because it bears the cost of any security incident but captures little of the benefit; workers conceal because disclosure invites extraction, a hold-up that strikes at the revelation of tacit knowledge, where neither ownership allocation nor contract design can reach it. What resolves both failures is not ownership form but the structure of observability. We prove an observability-asymmetry theorem: distributing the authority to observe and sanction extraction to a peer community, in the sense of Elinor Ostrom’s polycentric governance, sustains a no-extraction commitment under strictly weaker conditions than any bilateral-observability design. Contribution-based screening, peer-enforced commitment, and a community-governed repository implement the design. The model reframes shadow AI as a governance problem with an institutional rather than managerial solution, and identifies the organizational conditions under which that solution holds.

How Should Mentors Reduce Uncertainty? Theory and Evidence from Job-Seeker Training in France.
with Thomas Åstebro, Andrew Funck Bruno Crépon, Mona Mensmann, Mathis Schulte, Naja Pape, & (RCT on-going)
Presented at: AI Plus Management Consortium 2026 (UCL Management); CFXS 2025 (INSEAD, San Francisco)
Abstract Presentation

We develop a model of mentorship that centers on how mentors reduce uncertainty rather than how they transfer knowledge. Mentors do not merely add information; they filter and reorganize the mentee's decision environment, pruning dominated paths and transmitting heuristics for future evaluation. We formalize two distinct filtering mechanisms and show that they map onto the exploration-exploitation tradeoff at the level of the mentoring interaction. Under internal filtering, the mentor exposes the mentee to varied framings and prompts self-directed reasoning, building the mentee's own capacity to evaluate strategies. Under external filtering, the mentor delivers deterministic, high-confidence guidance, reducing ambiguity directly. We derive conditions under which each mechanism dominates for skill persistence after mentor removal and for mentees differing in initial self-regulatory capacity. We test these predictions in a randomized controlled trial with approximately 90,000 enrolled unemployed job seekers through France Travail, the French national employment agency. Participants in online soft-skills courses — Personal Initiative and Negotiation Skills — are randomly assigned to course content alone, content with an exploratory AI mentor, or content with a directive AI mentor. Both AI mentors share identical self-regulated learning infrastructure — prompting learners through cycles of planning, monitoring, and reflection — and identical course material; they differ only in the filtering mechanism. We cross-randomize across courses to test generalizability. Outcomes span course completion, knowledge retention, entrepreneurial skills, job search, venture creation, employment, income, and wellbeing, measured up to 18 months post-intervention.

The Calculus of AI Governance in Organizations
with Pablo Paniagua (Under review at Public Choice)
Abstract

Intra-organizational generative AI programs have so far delivered difficult-to-capture productivity gains. Employees, frustrated by restrictive tools, circumvent official policy via personal devices and consumer LLMs—a phenomenon known as “shadow AI”. We argue that shadow AI signals a hidden demand for a form of governance that hierarchical management cannot supply. Drawing on public choice, we develop a game-theoretic framework in which intra-organization AI adoption is a constitutional dilemma sustained by two governance challenges: (i) IT departments restrict access to AI not because restriction is efficient, but because of a Niskanen-style equilibrium in which a bureau faces personalized liability; and (ii) workers conceal AI-enabled productivity to protect those gains against intra-organization extraction, a Tullock–Tollison-style rent-extraction dynamic sustained by a credibility deficit. Building on the Bloomington tradition, we show that a polycentric structure resolves these challenges at the constitutional stage. The paper makes three contributions: first, a blame-avoidance dynamic we call the defensibility cascade, in which endogenous blame attribution makes restriction dominant; second, a discretion paradox, in which the discretion surrendered to charter a polycentric order is worthless in equilibrium; third, a characterization of how the accountability regime selects which organizational AI constitutions are feasible. The paper concludes by illuminating a broader public choice concern: the bureaucratic and rent-extractive incentives that distort AI regulation at the polity level reproduce themselves within organizations, and polycentric structures can serve as a remedy.

Can AI Replace Mentors? Evidence from Entrepreneurship Training in LATAM Schools.
with Igor Asanov, Thomas Åstebro, Emma Brunskill, Guido Buenstorf, Mona Mensmann, Lelys Dinarte-Diaz, & Adam Hardaker (Research Design Stage) [Grant Under Review: Volkswagen Stiftung, Change! Fellowship]
Abstract

Quality mentorship improves economic outcomes, but its reliance on scarce human capital limits scalability — particularly in developing economies. A fundamental question is whether the filtering and guidance functions of mentorship require a human relationship or whether they can be delivered algorithmically. We investigate this in a randomized controlled trial with 120,000 secondary students across Ecuador, Peru, and Chile. Students enrolled in online Personal Initiative courses are randomly assigned to course content alone, content augmented with an AI mentor, or content with access to trained professional mentors through a digital platform. Within the human mentoring arm, we randomize whether students observe mentor characteristics before selecting a mentor, generating exogenous variation in the information available for matching. This design separates the effect of mentorship itself from the effect of informed self-selection into mentors. We develop a framework distinguishing the informational function of mentorship — filtering uncertainty, transmitting heuristics — from its relational function, and derive conditions under which algorithmic delivery can substitute for human mentors. A secondary experiment randomizes whether follow-up surveys are conducted by AI voice agents or human interviewers, testing whether AI measurement reduces social desirability bias in noncognitive outcomes and estimating the cost-effectiveness of algorithmic data collection at scale. Outcomes include entrepreneurial skills, metacognitive capacity, and post-graduation employment and entrepreneurship.

El Costo Económico de la Delincuencia: Chile 2013-2022
with Leonardo Hernández & Jorge Alfaro (Published in Estudios Públicos, 2024)
Presented at: Chilean Public Policy Society 2023 (Santiago)
Abstract PDF DOI

This article examines the economic cost of crime in Chile in the past decade. Following the literature, we categorize the economic losses due to criminal activities into (a) anticipation costs, (b) consequence costs, and (c) response costs, and apply an accounting method to quantify the direct losses due to crime. We consider various types of crime-related costs such as expenses in security, insurance and medical treatments of victims, loss of labor income by victims and inmates, and the economic cost of deaths due to crime, among others. By relying on data from multiple sources such as tax records, government budgets, and official crime reports, we report a lower bound for the economic costs of crime in Chile of 2.1% of GDP in 2022, 46% higher than the cost (in percent of GDP) estimated for 2013 (90% higher if measured in USD of equal purchasing power). We find that in recent years there has been a significant increase in anticipation costs, mainly driven by the rise in security spending by the private sector. Results also show a significant growth in costs due to homicide and vehicle theft cases in recent years — the latter is also a cost borne by the private sector. The paper shows that the cost of crime as a percentage of GDP has increased steadily over the past decade, highlighting the need for a reassessment of security policies focusing on more effective prevention and response strategies. This research contributes to understanding the economic impact of crime in Chile and calls for policies addressing the evolving challenges posed by criminal activities.
