The Effect of Beliefs on Generative-AI Adoption by Entrepreneurs.
with Juan Pedro Ronconi [JMP] (Manuscript in preparation for Management Science)
Abstract IRB Pre-registration

Generative AI could help vulnerable entrepreneurs overcome information disadvantages, yet adoption remains minimal. Among Chilean microentrepreneurs—most without higher education or formal business training—AI tools remain largely unused despite their potential to democratize access to business expertise. We test whether beliefs about AI benefits explain this adoption gap through a randomized controlled trial. We randomize entrepreneurs into receiving information on the potential of GenAI as a business consultant or as a content creator, with a control group receiving neutral technical information. Through incentivized willingness-to-pay elicitation, we measure how information affects beliefs about GenAI's business value. We evaluate the impact of exogenously changing beliefs on GenAI take-up and on firm performance. Results inform whether low-cost information interventions can help reduce inequality in entrepreneurial success.

Platform Certification as a Substitute for Reputation: Evidence from Mandatory AI Disclosure.
with Michael Mödl (Submitted to the Strategic Entrepreneurship Journal)
Presented at: Academy of Management 2025 (Copenhagen); Belgian Entrepreneurship Research Day (U. Namur); MSI Doctoral Seminar (KU Leuven)
Abstract Presentation

This study examines how mandatory disclosure policies affect entrepreneurial resource acquisition when traditional credibility signals vary. We propose a conditional substitution framework where platform certification replaces missing reputational signals. Exploiting Kickstarter’s mandatory AI use disclosure policy as a quasi-natural experiment across 27,845 projects, we find that mandatory disclosure significantly enhances funding outcomes for AI-using entrepreneurs overall, though effects vary substantially by entrepreneur type. First-time entrepreneurs lacking track records experience positive effects, while serial entrepreneurs with established reputations experience negative effects. While prior work demonstrates that signals typically complement one another, we provide empirical evidence that platform verification’s value depends critically on entrepreneurs’ existing signal portfolios. These findings identify boundary conditions to signal complementarity and reveal how governance design democratizes market access.

Knowledge Leakage via Human Capital: How Contract Design Shapes Inventor Mobility in Licensing.
with Bart Leten, Stijn Kelchtermans, & Eduardo Melero (Manuscript in preparation for the Strategic Management Journal)
Presented at: European Policy for Intellectual Property (U. Antwerp); Summer School on Data and Algorithms for ST&I Studies (KU Leuven)
Abstract

Technology licensing creates a paradox for knowledge-based firms: monetizing intellectual property through outlicensing simultaneously exposes tacit knowledge holders—the inventors—to the licensee. Drawing on the knowledge-based view and signaling theory, we argue that licensing agreements signal inventor quality and, through clauses facilitating knowledge exchange, create organizational proximity that enables inventor poaching. Using machine learning to classify 340,000 clauses across 3,558 pharmaceutical licensing agreements, we find that outlicensing increases inventor mobility by 26–30 percentage points, with the dominant flow directed toward the licensee rather than the broader market. Crucially, contractual design moderates this effect heterogeneously: neither knowledge transfer provisions nor joint governance structures alone reduce within-dyad mobility, but their combination in relational contracts—where tacit knowledge exchange is embedded within shared decision-making structures—reduces mobility by 12–19 percentage points. This complementarity reveals that contractual mechanisms mitigating brain drain must simultaneously facilitate and govern knowledge transfer.

AI-Tutors and Entrepreneurial Skill Development: Evidence from Job-Seeker Training in France.
with Thomas Åstebro, Bruno Crépon, Mona Mensmann, Mathis Schulte, & Andrew Funck (Pilot/Validation Stage)
Presented at: Conference on Field Experiments in Strategy 2025 (San Francisco)
Abstract Presentation

Despite evidence that skills-based entrepreneurship training improves outcomes, scalability remains limited by reliance on expert facilitators. We test whether AI tutors employing self-regulated learning (SRL) pedagogical principles can replicate the benefits of expert coaching while fostering durable learning capabilities. In collaboration with France Travail (the French national employment agency), we conduct a large-scale randomized controlled trial with 460,000 eligible unemployed job seekers. Participants are assigned to online entrepreneurship courses (Personal Initiative or Negotiation Skills) delivered through three modalities: standard content only, content with a basic AI assistant, or content with an SRL-based AI coach. The SRL-based coach employs metacognitive scaffolding—prompting learners to articulate assumptions, test their reasoning, and reflect on their learning process—without providing direct answers. Through repeated metacognitive practice, learners internalize effective learning strategies that persist beyond the intervention. We hypothesize that SRL-based coaching will be most effective for participants with lower baseline self-regulatory capacity. Outcomes span from proximal measures (course completion, knowledge retention of entrepreneurial content) and learning process skills to long-term economic outcomes (venture creation, employment, income) measured 12-18 months post-intervention.

Scaling Soft Skills Training in Latin America in Secondary Schools.
with Igor Asanov, Thomas Åstebro, Emma Brunskill, Guido Buenstorf, Mona Mensmann, & Lelys Dinarte-Diaz (Full Proposal Stage, IPA Soft Skills Research Fund; Full Proposal Stage, JPAL Project AI Evidence)
Abstract

We evaluate the cost-effectiveness of AI coaching versus human mentorship for delivering entrepreneurial soft skills at scale in resource-constrained contexts. In a randomized controlled trial with 120,000 secondary students across Ecuador, Peru, and Chile, we compare digital Personal Initiative courses alone to courses augmented with either AI coaching or trained human mentors. Our AI implementation uses a dual large language model architecture where a second LLM judges each response to reduce hallucinations and ensure pedagogical alignment. We measure impacts on Personal Initiative, metacognitive skills, and post-graduation employment and entrepreneurial outcomes. The multi-country design enables us to assess heterogeneous treatment effects across diverse institutional contexts and estimate cost-effectiveness of alternative delivery models for human capital development in developing economies.

AI Training for Micro-Entrepreneurs.
with Juan Pedro Ronconi (Data Collection Stage)
Abstract IRB Pre-registration

Technology adoption among resource-constrained entrepreneurs remains low despite potential productivity gains. Traditional in-person training can improve outcomes but faces severe scalability constraints, while existing online courses show poor completion rates and limited effectiveness. We examine whether a cost-effective MOOC intervention integrating business practices with technological skills can achieve meaningful adoption and performance improvements at scale. In collaboration with ChileConverge, we conduct a randomized controlled trial with Chilean micro-entrepreneurs (N≈1,000) comparing a MOOC that teaches four core business practices—marketing, inventory management, record-keeping, and financial planning—with embedded instruction on using generative AI to implement these practices, against a pure control group. This integrated approach teaches business frameworks and technological tools as complementary capabilities rather than sequentially. We measure AI adoption (extensive and intensive margins), business practice implementation, and firm performance (revenue and profits) at baseline and 2-month follow-up. We test for heterogeneous treatment effects by gender, prior entrepreneurial experience, and baseline digital literacy. Our study addresses whether scalable online interventions can overcome both the quality limitations of traditional MOOCs and the cost barriers of in-person training, contributing to theories of technology adoption and training design in resource-constrained entrepreneurial contexts.

El Costo Económico de la Delincuencia: Chile 2013-2022
with Leonardo Hernández & Jorge Alfaro (Published in Estudios Públicos, 2024)
Presented at: Chilean Public Policy Society (Santiago)
Abstract PDF DOI

This article examines the economic cost of crime in Chile in the past decade. Following the literature, we categorize the economic losses due to criminal activities into (a) anticipation costs, (b) consequence costs, and (c) response costs, and apply an accounting method to quantify the direct losses due to crime. We consider various types of crime-related costs such as expenses in security, insurance and medical treatments of victims, loss of labor income by victims and inmates, and the economic cost of deaths due to crime, among others. By relying on data from multiple sources such as tax records, government budgets, and official crime reports, we report a lower bound for the economic costs of crime in Chile of 2.1% of GDP in 2022, 46% higher than the cost (in percent of GDP) estimated for 2013 (90% higher if measured in USD of equal purchasing power). We find that in recent years there has been a significant increase in anticipation costs, mainly driven by the rise in security spending by the private sector. Results also show a significant growth in costs due to homicide and vehicle theft cases in recent years — the latter is also a cost borne by the private sector. The paper shows that the cost of crime as a percentage of GDP has increased steadily over the past decade, highlighting the need for a reassessment of security policies focusing on more effective prevention and response strategies. This research contributes to understanding the economic impact of crime in Chile and calls for policies addressing the evolving challenges posed by criminal activities.
