Work in Progress

The Effect of Beliefs on Generative-AI Adoption by Entrepreneurs [Job Market Paper]
with Juan Pedro Ronconi

Abstract IRB Pre-registration PDF

We examine how information provision affects entrepreneurs' beliefs and technology adoption decisions through a randomized controlled trial with Chilean micro-entrepreneurs (N≈1,000). Participants received randomized information treatments about generative AI benefits: quantitative evidence (15% profit increase), qualitative examples of AI as business advisor, creative collaborator, or placebo information. Using incentive-compatible willingness-to-pay measures and instrumental variables design, we find information treatments significantly update beliefs about AI value and increase adoption intentions. Effects persist at 2-month follow-up, with heterogeneous responses by gender and prior technology experience. Results demonstrate that strategic information provision can overcome adoption barriers for productivity-enhancing technologies among resource-constrained entrepreneurs, offering implications for technology diffusion and entrepreneurial capability development in emerging markets.


From Choice to Mandate: Artificial Intelligence Disclosure as a Pseudo-Certification Scheme.
with Michael Mödl

Abstract PDF

The rapid adoption of AI has raised transparency concerns. Digital platforms have increasingly responded by mandating disclosure of AI use. Drawing on disclosure and certification theories, we suggest mandatory AI disclosure functions as a pseudo-certification mechanism, reducing information asymmetry and enhancing credibility. Analyzing 28,428 Kickstarter projects, we use conditional difference-in-differences focused on the period surrounding the platform's 2023 mandatory AI disclosure policy. Our analyses provide causal evidence that mandatory disclosure increases funding success probability by 13 percentage points for AI-disclosing projects. We explore moderating effects of project type and creator demographics. Our findings shed light on how platform governance shapes resource acquisition through verified transparency mechanisms.


Knowledge Leak Through Human Capital: How Contract Design Affects Inventor Mobility.
with Bart Leten, Stijn Kelchtermans, & Eduardo Melero

Abstract PDF

We examine an overlooked strategic challenge in biopharmaceutical technology licensing: how outlicensing affects inventor retention. Using 3,558 pharmaceutical licensing agreements (2000-2015) matched with patent data, we find inventors whose technologies are outlicensed exhibit 50% higher mobility rates than matched controls. This "brain drain" represents a hidden cost of licensing strategies in knowledge-intensive industries. We investigate whether relational contract design—characterized by collaborative provisions fostering ongoing partnership—can mitigate this effect. Using machine learning to classify contracts and staggered difference-in-differences estimation, we reveal how strategic IP decisions influence human capital outcomes. Our findings contribute to understanding how technology strategy and contract design shape innovation capabilities in the biopharmaceutical sector, where both knowledge transfer and talent retention are critical for competitive advantage.


AI-Tutors and Entrepreneurial Skill Development: Evidence from Job-Seeker Training in France.
with Thomas Åstebro, Mathis Schulte, Mona Mensmann, Bruno Crépon, & Andrew Funck

Abstract IRB Pre-registration PDF

This study investigates whether AI-enhanced metacognitive practice during entrepreneurial training creates durable improvements in entrepreneurial decision-making among unemployed job-seekers in France. We conduct a randomized controlled trial comparing standard entrepreneurial training (Personal Initiative and Negotiation Skills) with AI-augmented versions featuring retrieval-augmented generation chatbots designed as metacognitive coaches. Rather than providing information, these AI tutors guide learners through systematic hypothesis testing, assumption identification, and structured reflection using self-regulated learning frameworks. Our theoretical framework proposes that AI's value lies in intensifying metacognitive practice through personalized dialogue, consistent modeling, and active strategy generation. We expect stronger effects for participants with lower initial metacognitive abilities who acquire transferable problem-solving frameworks, moderated by prior AI familiarity and attitudes. We hypothesize that AI-enhanced training will improve both proximal metacognitive capabilities and distal venture performance outcomes, with effects persisting beyond technological support removal.

AI Training for Micro-Entrepreneurs
with Juan Pedro Ronconi

Abstract IRB Pre-registration PDF

We investigate whether integrated training combining business practices with generative AI skills enhances entrepreneurial performance and decision-making quality. Using a randomized controlled trial with Chilean micro-entrepreneurs (N≈1,000), we compare an intervention teaching core business practices (marketing, financial management, operations) alongside strategic AI prompting techniques against an active control group. Our design incorporates novel measures of economic rationality through Generalized Axiom of Revealed Preference (GARP) tests to assess decision-making quality improvements. Results show the combined intervention significantly increases business practice adoption, AI utilization, and objective performance metrics (sales, profits). Importantly, we find evidence that training enhances economic rationality, suggesting improved decision-making processes mediate performance gains. Effects exhibit heterogeneity by gender and entrepreneurial experience. These findings demonstrate that strategic capability-building interventions can simultaneously enhance both technological adoption and fundamental decision-making quality among resource-constrained entrepreneurs.

Pre-PhD Work

Balmaceda, M., Hernández, L., & Alfaro, J. (2024). El Costo Económico de la Delincuencia: Chile 2013-2022. Estudios Públicos, (175), 37-80. https://doi.org/10.38178/07183089/1143240217
Abstract PDF

This article examines the economic cost of crime in Chile in the past decade. Following the literature, we categorize the economic losses due to criminal activities into (a) anticipation costs, (b) consequence costs, and (c) response costs, and apply an accounting method to quantify the direct losses due to crime. We consider various types of crime-related costs such as expenses in security, insurance and medical treatments of victims, loss of labor income by victims and inmates, and the economic cost of deaths due to crime, among others. By relying on data from multiple sources such as tax records, government budgets, and official crime reports, we report a lower bound for the economic costs of crime in Chile of 2.1% of GDP in 2022, 46% higher than the cost (in percent of GDP) estimated for 2013 (90% higher if measured in USD of equal purchasing power). We find that in recent years there has been a significant increase in anticipation costs, mainly driven by the rise in security spending by the private sector. Results also show a significant growth in costs due to homicide and vehicle theft cases in recent years — the latter is also a cost borne by the private sector. The paper shows that the cost of crime as a percentage of GDP has increased steadily over the past decade, highlighting the need for a reassessment of security policies focusing on more effective prevention and response strategies. This research contributes to understanding the economic impact of crime in Chile and calls for policies addressing the evolving challenges posed by criminal activities.
